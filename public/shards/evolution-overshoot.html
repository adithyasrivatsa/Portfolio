OVERSHOOTS. or: self-awareness was probably a bug, not a feature

Every other animal on Earth has the decency to just exist. Humans invented existential dread, geopolitics, and the ability to feel bad about eating a sandwich. We need to talk.

01 · OriginThe Perfectly Adequate Ape

About 300,000 years ago, there was an ape. He was fine. Not spectacular, not the fastest, not the strongest. He lived, he ate, he reproduced, he died. The savanna didn't care about him particularly. He didn't care about the savanna philosophically. This was a stable and functional arrangement.

Then something happened in the prefrontal cortex. A mutation, a threshold crossed, a feedback loop that didn't stop when it should have. The ape got smarter. Then smarter. Then way too smart for his own good.

He started noticing patterns. Then noticing that he was noticing. Then asking why he was noticing, and whether the noticing had a purpose, and whether purpose itself was real, and whether anything was real, and whether the question of whether anything was real was itself real, and around 2am he discovered existential dread and never fully recovered.

This is us. We are that ape, still sitting in that spiral, still 2am, still slightly confused about what's happening.

"The capacity to be aware of your own awareness is either the greatest gift in the known universe or the most spectacular own-goal in the history of biology. The jury — composed entirely of self-aware beings — has a significant conflict of interest."

— this essay, right now

02 · The BugSelf-Awareness Is a Ridiculous Thing to Have

Let's be precise about what self-awareness actually is, because we say it like it's obviously good and I want to push on that.

Self-awareness means: you have a model of yourself running inside yourself. A simulation of you, within you, which you observe, judge, compare to other simulated yous, compare to idealized yous you invented, compare to yous you perceive other people perceiving, and then feel feelings about all of those comparisons simultaneously. At all times. While also trying to eat breakfast.

No other animal does this at this resolution. A dog knows if it's hungry. It doesn't lie awake wondering if it's the kind of dog that deserves to be hungry, whether its hunger reflects some deeper inadequacy, whether other dogs think about hunger more productively, or whether the concept of hunger is itself a social construct.

We do all of that. About everything.

The cognitive overhead of self-awareness is enormous. You are burning processing power, every waking hour, maintaining a story about who you are, defending that story, updating that story, mourning older versions of the story, worrying about future versions of the story. This is expensive. This is, from a pure energy-expenditure standpoint, deeply irrational.

Every other organism uses that energy to, I don't know, survive. We use it to feel complicated about things that already happened three years ago.

03 · The FeatureBut It Also Built Cathedrals. So.

I should be fair. The same overshoot that gave us anxiety gave us everything else.

The same recursive self-model that produces existential dread also produces: mathematics, music, grief (which means we loved someone deeply enough to miss them), literature, architecture, the scientific method, the ability to postpone present satisfaction for future gain, moral philosophy, and the fact that I can sit here and write an essay analyzing the mechanism that allowed me to write this essay.

The same brain. The exact same cognitive architecture that invented vaccines invented bioweapons. That wrote the Mahabharata wrote propaganda. That built hospitals built concentration camps. That looked at the night sky and felt wonder also looked at the night sky and decided those stars belonged to their kingdom.

Evolution didn't give us a good brain or a bad brain. It gave us a powerful brain with no fixed purpose, attached to the same fear, status-hunger, and tribalism it always had. A sports car engine bolted onto a donkey cart. The horsepower is real. The steering is the original steering. This is the configuration we're working with.

04 · The DefenceWhy Most People Being Dumb Is Brilliant

Here's the argument I want to make seriously, because I think it's actually true and people laugh it off too quickly: mass cognitive mediocrity is a civilizational stabilizer.

Think about what a population of uniformly high-intelligence, high-self-awareness individuals would actually produce. Everyone questioning the default. Everyone stress-testing authority. Everyone running their own cost-benefit analysis on social norms. Everyone asking "but why should I, specifically, follow this rule if I can calculate my way around it."

That's not a civilization. That's a coordination nightmare. That's every institution collapsing under the weight of its own critical mass. That's game theory eating itself.

The Uncomfortable Thesis

Most people operating on instinct, habit, social conformity, and unexamined assumption is not a bug in human civilization. It is the load-bearing wall. The examined life, Socrates famously insisted, is the only one worth living. Socrates was executed. The unexamined civilization kept running.

The anthropologist Joseph Henrich has a framework for this: humans are not intelligent because of individual cognition. We are intelligent because of cumulative culture — the ability to store and transmit solutions across generations without every individual having to re-derive them. Most of what you know, you inherited without examination. How to hold a fork, how to queue, what counts as rudeness, which plants are edible. You didn't figure any of that out. You absorbed it.

That absorption works best when people trust the system and don't constantly interrogate it. The skeptic who questions everything is epistemically noble and practically paralyzed. The person who follows inherited wisdom without examining it keeps the buses running.

Both types are necessary. The ratio we have — a lot of the second, a few of the first — is probably not accidental. It's the configuration that doesn't immediately collapse.

05 · The VerdictPerfection or Mistake? Wrong Question.

The question "are humans a perfection of the universe or a mistake" assumes the universe has preferences. It doesn't. Evolution doesn't have preferences either. It has outcomes. The outcome called Homo sapiens is not optimized for anything in particular — not happiness, not longevity, not even survival in any cosmic sense. It is optimized, loosely, for reproduction in the Pleistocene savanna. We are now running on hardware built for that environment inside conditions that are almost nothing like it.

We are, in the most literal sense, a biological system operating outside its design specifications.

Is that a mistake? Or is the fact that we can notice this — can sit here, in our cities, eating food we didn't hunt, writing essays on machines we built with the brains that were supposed to be watching for lions — is that itself the most remarkable accident in four billion years of biological history?

The honest answer is: both. At the same time. Neither cancels the other.

Self-awareness is ridiculous. It causes immeasurable suffering. It also means that somewhere in the universe, matter is looking at matter and asking what it is. That has never happened anywhere else we know of. Not once. Not in four billion years of life on this planet and, as far as we can tell, not anywhere in the observable universe.

We are the universe's one known attempt at understanding itself. The attempt is chaotic, frequently cruel, badly coordinated, and probably temporary. But it is the only attempt there is. The fact that it comes packaged with social anxiety and tribalism and the capacity to feel bad about eating a sandwich — that's just the price of the ticket.

The majority being "dumb" — operating on instinct, on inheritance, on unexamined assumption — keeps the lights on. The minority who can't stop examining everything moves the whole thing forward, even when they're insufferable about it.

You're probably reading this because you're in the second group. Welcome. It comes with a higher baseline of discomfort and a significantly better view.

Whether that trade is worth it is, characteristically, something only you can decide. And you will, probably at 2am, probably more times than is strictly necessary.

That's the overshoot. That's the whole thing.
